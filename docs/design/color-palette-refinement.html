One accent isn't a palette — what Pond needs before it ships

The first document picked a hue. This one asks the next question: is a single accent enough for what this app is about to become? The Dashboard epic (#6) puts two data families on screen at once — weight and calories — plus a goal line, and the mood system (#17) makes the palette a living system rather than a one-time choice. Both expose the same gap: none of the three candidates says what happens when one hue has to do more than color a button.

What the current three options don't answer

  • Charts need a second hue, and nobody assigned it. With a single accent, #6's weight line, calorie bars, and goal overlay either all inherit the same teal (unreadable), or each chart invents its own color outside the system (incoherent). This decision is cheaper to make now, on paper, than mid-epic.
  • The moods share no constant. #17 lets the user switch Dusk/Sage/Pond, but as specified, switching re-colors everything — including data visualizations. A weight line that's violet on Monday and green on Tuesday makes the user re-learn their own charts. Some layer of the palette should hold still while the mood moves.
  • All three candidates are cool or botanical. Dusk, Sage, and Pond sit within one temperature band. That's a missed register for a product whose mascot is a sun-warmed animal — and it means the mood switcher offers three variations on the same feeling rather than three feelings.
  • The neutral-tinting rule was shown but never written. Each specimen in the first doc quietly tinted its grays toward its accent (Pond's dark background is #0f181c, not #131518). That's the right move — zero-chroma gray next to a colored accent is what made the current app feel unfinished — but it exists only as pixels, not as a rule #17 can implement.

My recommendation. Not a new hue — Pond, finished. The teal keeps every role it already won (buttons, focus, active nav, weight data). A warm river-sand joins as the counterpoint: calorie data, secondary highlights, the occasional warm tint on a stat card. Where the pond meets its bank. Plus: the two-hue split gives #6's charts a permanent, meaningful assignment — weight is always water, calories are always sand — and the warmth stops an all-teal UI from feeling clinical. Watch for: sand is a fill and data color, never a second "brand" — if both hues fight for buttons and links, the calm is gone. One rule: interactive = teal, informational-warm = sand.

Rules that hold across every mood

This is the piece the first document was missing: the parts of the palette that don't move when the mood does. Writing them down now is what keeps #17 from becoming four unrelated themes in a trench coat.

Layer Rule Why
Neutrals Every gray (background, borders, muted text) is tinted 2–4% toward the active mood's accent — never zero-chroma. Untinted gray beside a colored accent is what made the shipped app feel unfinished. The tint is the difference between "gray app with a colored button" and a palette.
Chart identity Weight = the mood's accent. Calories = the shared warm sand. Goal line = dashed neutral. Constant across moods. Users re-read the same charts daily; the data's color is part of its meaning and shouldn't reset when the mood does.
Warm counterpoint The sand family (#c9a878 fill / #8a6a3c text, lightened on dark) is shared by all moods, including Tortoise — there it simply sits closer to the primary. One warm constant ties the moods together and keeps the calorie data recognizable everywhere.
Semantics Destructive stays the existing muted red, used for irreversible actions only — never for data, never for a missed target. No semantic green anywhere. Brief §2: a missed week is shown factually, not as a failure state. The palette enforces what the copy promises.
Interaction Only the mood accent is interactive (buttons, links, focus, active nav). Sand never is. Two hues that both look clickable double the visual noise; one interactive hue keeps the calm.

The final token map — every mood, all five slots

To be unambiguous: Riverbank is not a sixth mood. It's the system every mood implements — an accent pair, the shared sand for calories, and tinted grounds. Pond remains the default mood; Dusk and Sage need no new hue work, they simply inherit the sand family like everyone else. Tortoise is the one exception: its dark-mode calorie color drops to bark-brown because its amber accent already lives in sand's neighborhood.

Whether the shipped roster is all five or the tighter four (dropping Sage — see below), each mood that ships uses exactly this five-slot structure, so #17's implementation is one token table per mood, nothing bespoke.

My take

Riverbank as the system, Tortoise as the fourth mood

Don't replace the Pond decision — complete it. Riverbank (D) is Pond plus the answers to the questions #6 and #17 were about to force: a second, warm, judgment-free hue with a fixed job, and a written set of cross-mood rules. Tortoise (E) then earns its place in the mood switcher by being the one warm register the current trio lacks — a real mood change, not a hue swap. Lagoon (F) is the strongest "bright" candidate if the roster wants one — but note it overlaps Pond in hue, and Sage overlaps it in the nature-green space; a four-mood roster of Pond · Dusk · Tortoise · Lagoon covers calm/cool/warm/bright with less redundancy than shipping all five. Practically: fold the sand family and the cross-mood rules table into #17's implementation notes, and let #6 build its charts against the chart-identity rule from day one instead of retrofitting it.

Nothing on this page touches the repo's source. It refines, rather than replaces, docs/design/color-palette-options.html — Dusk, Sage, and Pond stand as specified there; this document adds the sand counterpoint, the Tortoise and Lagoon mood candidates, and the cross-mood rules. If the direction is agreed, the concrete next step is a comment on #17 (final mood roster + the rules table) and one on #6 (chart hue assignments), keeping implementation issue-first as always.
